# Maarova Methodology Brief: Research Foundations for the Next Iteration

Working document. The aim is to move Maarova from a Hofstede-anchored first-generation African psychometric tool to a defensible, audit-resistant assessment system grounded in current cross-cultural psychometric research. Each section ends with the specific implication for the Maarova product.

---

## Part 1: Why the Hofstede frame is not enough

The Hofstede scores most consultancies still quote for Nigeria come from IBM employee surveys conducted between 1967 and 1973, published in 1980. The Nigeria number was actually derived from a three-country West African aggregate (Nigeria, Ghana, Sierra Leone), not a Nigeria-specific sample. Iguisi's 2018 cement-industry replication confirmed Nigeria scores higher than the West African aggregate on most dimensions, that Nigerian collectivism is sticky but drifting toward individualism, and that power distance has barely moved.

That data has three problems for vetting work. The sample is 50 years old and was never Nigeria-specific. The masculinity dimension has been renamed by Hofstede's successors at The Culture Factor Group because the gendered framing no longer holds up, which means the "Nigeria scores 60 on masculinity" figure now reads as a measurement of achievement orientation conflated with role rigidity that has since uncoupled. And the national score masks substantial ethnic, generational, and urban-rural variance that is more predictive of actual workplace behavior than any country-level average.

The implication for Maarova is not to abandon Hofstede. It is to demote Hofstede from load-bearing framework to background literature acknowledged in the methodology section. Anyone sophisticated who interrogates Maarova will know the dataset is half a century old. The defensive answer cannot be "we are using Hofstede." It has to be "we treat Hofstede as one input alongside more recent and methodologically stronger frameworks, and our calibration comes from primary data on Nigerian executives."

## Part 2: The frameworks that should replace it as load-bearing

### HEXACO and the Honesty-Humility factor

The HEXACO model is the strongest available personality framework for integrity prediction. The model adds a sixth factor beyond the Big Five called Honesty-Humility, comprising four facets: sincerity, fairness, greed avoidance, and modesty. Across multiple meta-analyses, Honesty-Humility outperforms every Big Five dimension as a predictor of counterproductive work behavior, workplace deviance, ethical decision-making, and integrity-related outcomes. The incremental validity over the Big Five is meaningful, with effect sizes that justify the additional measurement burden.

For Nigerian context specifically, HEXACO has been validated in African samples. Lee, Ogunfowora, and Ashton (2005) tested HEXACO against the Supernumerary Personality Inventory and found that HEXACO accommodated integrity-related variance more effectively than the Big Five. A 572-person study at three public universities in southwestern Nigeria examined Honesty-Humility as a moderator between organizational justice and workplace deviance, confirming the construct's predictive utility in Nigerian working populations.

The practical implication is that Maarova's integrity scale should be HEXACO-anchored, not Big Five-anchored. If the current Maarova architecture uses Big Five constructs for integrity vetting, that is a significant methodological gap. The fix is not cosmetic. It requires reworking the item pool around the four Honesty-Humility facets and validating each facet against integrity-relevant criteria in a Nigerian sample.

### SAPI as the African-context refinement

The South African Personality Inventory is the most rigorous indigenously developed African personality assessment in existence. It was built using an emic-etic approach across all 11 official South African languages, starting from how native speakers actually describe personality in their own tongues rather than translating Western inventories. The resulting model has nine factors, of which two are genuinely beyond what the Big Five or HEXACO capture: Relationship Harmony and Facilitating. These reflect social-relational personality content that emerges strongly in collectivist cultural settings and barely appears in Western lexical studies.

The SAPI Integrity factor is particularly relevant for Maarova. It correlates with HEXACO Honesty-Humility but with a different emphasis. Where HEXACO Honesty-Humility weights greed avoidance and modesty heavily, the SAPI Integrity factor emphasizes fairness and resistance to discrimination. This is not a small distinction in the African context. Nigerian executive integrity failures often look less like greed in isolation and more like in-group favoritism, ethnic patronage, and selective application of rules to favored parties. The SAPI integrity content captures that pattern better than the HEXACO content alone.

The practical implication is that Maarova should run a hybrid construct architecture. HEXACO provides the universally validated backbone with strong cross-cultural literature behind it. SAPI provides the social-relational content (Relationship Harmony, Facilitating) and the fairness-weighted Integrity emphasis that fits African working contexts. This is not a methodological compromise. It is the current state of the art for African personality assessment, and the SAPI team at North-West University and University of Pretoria has published the validation work to defend it.

### GLOBE practice-vs-values gap as the response-bias framework

The GLOBE Phase 2 data, collected on 17,000 middle managers across 62 countries including Nigeria, did something Hofstede did not. It measured each cultural dimension twice: once as "as is" practice (what people actually do) and once as "should be" values (what people think the culture should be). The gap between these two scores for any given country is methodologically explosive for assessment work.

Wanasika et al's 2011 analysis of the Sub-Saharan Africa cluster, which included Nigeria, found large gaps on Humane Orientation, Gender Egalitarianism, Performance Orientation, Institutional Collectivism, and Future Orientation. Respondents reported their current cultural practice but indicated strong aspirational pull toward different values. In-Group Collectivism was the highest practice score in the cluster. Power Distance was high in practice and people did not particularly want it lowered.

This is the response-bias framework Maarova needs to internalize. When a Nigerian executive completes a self-report integrity instrument, the practice-values gap predicts which items will pull responses toward the aspirational pole rather than the lived-experience pole. Items touching humane orientation, fairness, future orientation, and institutional cohesion will be biased toward "should be" answers. Items touching in-group collectivism and power distance will be answered closer to the practice pole. The Maarova methodology should map every item to a GLOBE dimension and flag items with high practice-values gap as candidates for forced-choice formatting or behavioral evidence supplementation.

This also gives Maarova a defensible academic citation when explaining why self-report alone is inadequate for the Nigerian context. "We follow Wanasika et al's GLOBE-derived practice-values gap analysis to identify items where social desirability bias is predictably elevated" is the kind of sentence that survives an audit by a sophisticated buyer.

### Dark Triad and Dark Tetrad

The Dark Triad (Machiavellianism, narcissism, psychopathy) and the more recent Dark Tetrad (adding sadism) are the standard malevolent personality constructs in current organizational psychology. They predict counterproductive work behavior, unethical decisions, abusive supervision, and white-collar crime tendencies. For executive vetting specifically, they are arguably more diagnostic than HEXACO Honesty-Humility on its own, because Honesty-Humility tells you about a person's baseline ethical orientation while Dark Triad tells you about active manipulation, exploitation, and exploitation-rationalization tendencies.

The standard instruments are the Short Dark Triad (SD3) and the Short Dark Tetrad (SD4), both publicly available and validated. They are short enough to embed in a Maarova battery without significantly extending administration time. The methodological risk is faking. Executives undergoing vetting know what Dark Triad items are detecting, so direct self-report is heavily faked. This is where forced-choice format and indirect measurement methods become essential rather than optional.

The practical implication is that Maarova should include Dark Triad or Dark Tetrad coverage, but with format and validity-indicator protections that account for the high faking incentive in vetting contexts.

## Part 3: The methodological architecture

### Response format: quasi-ipsative forced-choice with Thurstonian IRT scoring

The standard Likert-type rating scale is the worst possible response format for executive vetting in high-stakes contexts. It is vulnerable to acquiescence bias, central tendency bias, extreme response style, faking, and social desirability. Forced-choice formats, where respondents must rank or select between items rather than rate them independently, mitigate these biases substantially. The meta-analytic evidence from Salgado and others shows that quasi-ipsative forced-choice formats predict job performance better than Likert formats for personnel selection across occupational groups.

The historical objection to forced-choice has been that pure ipsative scores have psychometric problems for inter-individual comparison. This objection has been largely resolved by Thurstonian Item Response Theory (TIRT) scoring methods, which extract normative information from forced-choice responses and allow valid comparison between candidates. The current state of the art is quasi-ipsative blocks scored with TIRT, with items matched on social desirability within each block so that no answer is obviously the "right" one.

For Maarova, this means the response format itself becomes a defensible methodological choice. A forced-choice TIRT-scored battery is harder to fake, harder to game with social desirability, and provides more discriminating information for ranking candidates than any Likert instrument can. The implementation cost is significant. Item development requires social-desirability matching within blocks, scoring requires TIRT estimation, and the platform needs to handle the scoring complexity. But the cost is one-time, and the resulting product is meaningfully more defensible than the standard Likert questionnaire most competitors use.

### Item content beyond self-report

Self-report items in any format have ceiling validity. A vetting battery should triangulate across multiple item content types to reduce shared method variance.

Situational Judgement Tests (SJTs) present candidates with realistic workplace scenarios and ask them to select or rank response options. They are context-specific, harder to fake than direct trait items, and can be designed to surface the exact integrity dilemmas Maarova is trying to detect. For Nigerian healthcare leadership specifically, SJT scenarios can include patronage requests, billing irregularities, regulator interactions, family pressure to favor in-group candidates, and procurement conflicts of interest. The content can be developed from real cases C4A has encountered, which is itself a competitive moat.

Conditional Reasoning Tests (CRTs), developed by Lawrence James and colleagues, are the most underused integrity measurement method in commercial assessment. They present items that look like reasoning problems but are actually designed to detect implicit justification mechanisms. A respondent high in aggression-related justification will select different "logical" answers than someone without those implicit structures, without realizing the test is measuring personality. CRTs have been validated for aggression, achievement motivation, and integrity-relevant constructs. The development cost is high, but a small bank of well-validated CRT items adds a measurement source that is essentially fake-proof.

Biographical data items (biodata) ask about past behavior in specific contexts. "Have you ever discovered a discrepancy in financial records you were responsible for? What did you do?" with structured response options. Biodata is highly predictive of future behavior, particularly for integrity outcomes, because past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior. The methodological challenge is scoring, which requires empirical keying against criterion samples, but once developed, biodata scales are robust.

The practical implication is that Maarova should not be a single-format instrument. The vetting product should triangulate forced-choice personality (HEXACO + SAPI + Dark Triad), context-specific SJTs, a small CRT module for implicit measurement, and a biodata section. The total administration time can be kept under 90 minutes with careful design.

### Validity indicators and faking detection

Every assessment used in high-stakes selection should include explicit faking detection. The classical approach is a social desirability scale embedded in the battery, scored separately, that flags candidates whose response pattern is statistically improbable. The Paulhus Balanced Inventory of Desirable Responding distinguishes self-deceptive enhancement from impression management, which matters because the former reflects an honestly held but inflated self-view while the latter reflects deliberate misrepresentation.

Beyond social desirability scales, response-pattern indicators detect inconsistency (answering similar items differently), extreme responding, careless responding (random patterns), and over-positive presentation. These are cheap to implement and provide audit-trail defensibility. A Maarova report that includes a "validity indicators" section showing the candidate's response was within normal parameters is significantly more defensible in a contested hiring outcome than one without.

The practical implication is that validity indicators should be a standard module in every Maarova administration, not an optional add-on. The cost of including them is low. The cost of not having them when a hiring decision is challenged in court is potentially catastrophic.

### Cross-source 360 reputation module

Hogan's research program established that what others think of you is more predictive of actual workplace behavior than what you think of yourself. The "view from outside" is less subject to self-deception and impression management because the rater has no incentive to misrepresent the candidate. For executive vetting in particular, where the candidate has every incentive to manage their self-presentation, third-party reputation evidence weighted appropriately can shift the assessment substantially.

A Maarova 360 module would solicit structured ratings from former colleagues, subordinates, supervisors, and where appropriate, suppliers, regulators, and patients. The ratings would map to the same construct framework as the self-report battery, allowing direct comparison between self-view and reputation. Discrepancies are themselves diagnostic: a candidate who rates themselves highly on integrity facets that their former subordinates rate poorly is exhibiting either self-deception, impression management, or both.

The practical implementation challenge is that 360 evidence in Nigerian executive contexts will hit the in-group collectivism factor head-on. Former colleagues will often refuse to give honest negative ratings because of relational obligation. This is a known problem with culture-aware solutions: forced-choice 360 items, anonymized aggregation thresholds, comparison to industry baselines rather than absolute scoring, and behavior-anchored items rather than trait items all reduce the in-group protection bias.

### Sampling, norms, and stratification

This is where Maarova's defensibility ultimately stands or falls. No instrument is valid in the abstract. It is valid against a specific normative sample. The current state of cross-cultural assessment in Nigeria is that almost every commercially used instrument is normed against samples that bear no relationship to the Nigerian executive population. Maarova's competitive position depends on having actual Nigerian executive norm tables, stratified by the variables that genuinely matter.

The minimum defensible stratification is generation (under 40, 40-55, over 55), urbanity (Lagos-Abuja-PH metro versus other), sector experience (clinical, administrative, financial, regulatory), and possibly ethnicity, though the last is politically and methodologically fraught. The sample size needs to support measurement invariance testing across at least the first two strata. A pilot validation sample of 400-600 executives stratified across these variables is the floor. Anything less is statistically inadequate for measurement invariance work, which is itself a defensibility requirement.

The cost of this sampling work is non-trivial. Realistic estimate: N40-80M for a properly designed validation study including incentives, administration, expert panel review, and analysis. This becomes Maarova's primary intellectual property. Anyone can buy HEXACO items. No one else has the Nigerian executive norm tables. That is the moat.

### Measurement invariance and differential item functioning

Cross-cultural assessment requires measurement invariance testing. The technical requirement is that an item must measure the same construct in the same way across groups for between-group comparisons to be valid. If the Honesty-Humility scale operates differently for Yoruba versus Igbo respondents, or for under-40 versus over-40 respondents, then comparing scores across these groups is meaningless. Measurement invariance is tested in multiple stages: configural (same factor structure), metric (same loadings), scalar (same item intercepts), and strict (same residuals).

Differential Item Functioning (DIF) testing operates at the individual item level. An item shows DIF if respondents from different groups with the same underlying trait level have different probabilities of endorsing it. Items with significant DIF are biased and should be revised or dropped.

For Maarova, neither measurement invariance nor DIF testing is optional. They are the technical foundation for claiming the instrument works fairly across the population it is administered to. Without them, any contested hiring outcome where the candidate was from a minority ethnic group exposes Maarova to a discrimination claim that the instrument cannot rebut.

The implementation requires the validation sample described above, plus access to psychometric software (Mplus, R packages lavaan and mirt, or equivalent) and analyst capability. This is specialist work, not generalist data science. C4A should partner with a psychometrician for the validation phase rather than attempting it in-house.

### Audit trail and defensibility documentation

Every Maarova assessment output should include a defensibility appendix that documents the methodological choices, validation evidence, and limitations of the specific report. For a vetting product, this is not optional academic flourish. It is the document that protects C4A and the client from challenge.

The appendix should include: which constructs were assessed, which instruments and item types were used, the validation sample characteristics, measurement invariance evidence for the candidate's demographic profile, validity indicator results for this administration, comparison to relevant norm groups, and a clear statement of what the assessment does and does not claim to predict. The appendix becomes the standard format. The candidate report stays accessible to non-specialist hiring decision-makers. The appendix stays available for sophisticated review.

This is also where Maarova differentiates from commodity psychometric services. A boutique vetting product that takes documentation and defensibility seriously is priced and positioned very differently from a self-service personality questionnaire.

## Part 4: Maarova upgrade vectors, prioritized

The full transition to the methodology described above is a 12-18 month program. Within that, sequence matters. The following prioritization reflects what creates the most defensibility per unit of effort.

The first ninety days should focus on construct foundation and item architecture. Audit the current Maarova construct framework against HEXACO and SAPI. Identify which existing items map cleanly to HEXACO Honesty-Humility facets, which map to SAPI Integrity and social-relational factors, and which are orphaned or measuring constructs that should be deprecated. Draft a unified construct map. Develop the first version of forced-choice blocks for the Honesty-Humility facets with social-desirability-matched items. This work is methodologically heavy but does not require new field data, so it can run on existing team capacity.

The second ninety days should focus on response format and validity indicators. Build the TIRT scoring infrastructure. Embed a Paulhus BIDR-equivalent social desirability scale and response-pattern indicators. Develop the first SJT module of 20-30 scenarios drawn from C4A case experience, with expert panel scoring keys. Pilot the SJT module on a small convenience sample (50-100 respondents) to identify items that don't discriminate. This phase produces the first version of a meaningfully upgraded Maarova that can be used commercially while the larger validation work continues.

Months six to twelve should focus on the validation sample. Recruit 400-600 Nigerian executives stratified appropriately. Administer the full battery. Conduct measurement invariance and DIF analysis. Build the norm tables. Publish a methodology paper, either in a peer-reviewed African psychology journal or as a Maarova white paper with the methodology section open for inspection. This phase creates the actual intellectual property moat.

Months twelve to eighteen should focus on the 360 reputation module, the conditional reasoning module, and ongoing validation against criterion data. The CRT module is high-cost development but high-value differentiation. The 360 module requires platform engineering as well as methodology design. Criterion validation against actual workplace outcomes (promotion, termination, ethics violations, performance ratings) is the gold standard for predictive validity claims and requires longitudinal data collection.

Across all phases, the documentation discipline should be continuous. Every methodological choice gets written down with its rationale and its citation backing. The audit trail is built as the product is built, not retrofitted.

## Part 5: What this means for the current Maarova codebase

The likely current state of Maarova, based on typical first-generation African psychometric products and the framework hints in the existing C4A materials, is:

- Construct foundation anchored in Hofstede cultural dimensions with possibly some Big Five overlay
- Likert-type response format with 5 or 7 point scales
- Self-report only, no 360 or behavioral evidence integration
- Scoring algorithms based on simple summed scales without IRT
- Either no validity indicators or basic ones not derived from current literature
- No measurement invariance testing
- Norm references either to international samples or to a small Nigerian convenience sample, not a stratified executive validation sample
- Methodology documentation that names the framework but does not defend specific psychometric properties

If even half of that is accurate, the gap between current Maarova and the framework described above is large enough that the upgrade is functionally a v2.0, not a refinement. The good news is that v2.0 is genuinely competitive, genuinely defensible, and genuinely a moat. The current v1.0 is likely vulnerable to any sophisticated buyer who asks the obvious questions.
